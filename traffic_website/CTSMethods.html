Research Questions, Methodology, and Findings

In our study of Chicago traffic patterns, we originally set out to discover whether traffic cameras had an affect on the number of accidents within a city. We selected Chicago as the city we would examine based on the publicly available information it provided in its APIs. In an ideal world, we would be able to compare the number of accidents prior to traffic camera implementation with the number of accidents afterwards to determine if there was a correlation between the amount of traffic cameras and the number of accidents in a given area. Unfortunately, this information was not available to us. As a result, we focused on discovering a correlation between the number of traffic cameras and the amount of accidents from 2016 to March 2019.

To compare traffic cameras with accidents, we used a series of regions provided by the city of Chicago. Our datasets provided latitude and longitude for each region, each accident, and each camera, and we assigned a numerical region to each crash and each camera. We could then determine that there was a positive correlation between traffic cameras and accidents.

We had some concerns that this correlation was due to a kind of reverse causality; there were more cameras in areas where traffic accidents were more common, rather than the other way around. In order to assess this, we tried various methods of normalizing our data to account for differences in regional traffic flows. One of these methods involved not assessing the number of accidents, but the rate of injuries and fatalities those accidents produced. Using this method, we were able to determine that the presence of traffic cameras had no correlation with injury and fatality rate per accident.

The datasets we used contained a wealth of information not related to our original research question. I've included some of the more interesting visualizations here as well. One of them shows the regional prevalence of accidents caused by drunk driving.
